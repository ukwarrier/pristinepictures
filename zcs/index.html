What should our goal be when replying to users?

Respond in way to make the customer feel that they are important (they really are) and their opinions are valued. The response must carry a tone that give customer the confidence that their issues are heard and will be resolved - this is very important to earn trust.

 

Never keep them hanging

  • Respond quickly even if you do not have the solution at hand.
  • Get back to them as soon as when you have one.
  • When you say you come back with a solution in a few hours, mark the email for “to-do” and add it to your calendar for reminder - Never give them a chance to remind you that they are waiting to hear from you.

 

Use Simple, informative content and polite language

  • Let them know that you are glad to receive their email.
  • Set a polite, gentle, elegant tone.
  • Avoid jargons in your communication with customers.
  • Be personal in addressing the customer.
  • If you are in a situation to give them instructions to resolve an issue, send out step-by-step (concise) instructions.
  •  Make sure your instructions are clear and easy to understand (Don’t make them think too much)

 

Close out on a friendly note

  • Say thanks and you are glad to assist them.
  • Provide contact information (if not your mobile number, other means to contact you depending on the criticality of the incident) in the signature.
  • Last, not the least, run spellcheck / grammar check before hitting the send button.

 

Follow up

  • Own the responsibility.
  • After you resolve the issue and you don’t hear back from them, follow up to check whether or not the fix/resolution worked.
  • If it did, let them know you are glad and set the status of the ticket to ‘resolved’.
  • If not, follow up with team till resolution while keeping the customer updated on the status and ETA.
  • Make sure to add your comments while the resolution is in progress.
  • If the issue is resolved, make sure the ticket is closed.

How would you explain what an API is to someone who has never heard of one?

Think of API as a “user interface” that different programs use to talk with each other. Assume your boss wants you to schedule a meeting for him on his behalf. You then login to your scheduling app (read webex), set the time, add all required participants and send the invite to everyone. Based on the number of users who accepted or rejected the invite, you let your boss know of tentative participation. You were given a task to perform, you understand what are you supposed to do, then you completed the task and returned the results.

 

In this analogy, you acted as an interface between your boss and your scheduling app. The app cannot take verbal orders from your boss - but you may :) You know “how to” set up time using webex app and your boss tell you “when, where and who should attend the meeting” - you scheduled the meeting.

 

API is a set of commands that you can use (or available) with a program to communicate with another programs - APIs can be web based or platform specific.
